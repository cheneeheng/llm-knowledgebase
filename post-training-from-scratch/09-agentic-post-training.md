# Chapter 9 — Agentic Post-Training

## 9.1 Conceptual picture

An agent interleaves model turns with *environment* turns: emit a tool call, receive an observation, think, act again — for dozens or hundreds of steps — until a task completes. Training this is RLVR with three things changed at once: the unit of experience is a **trajectory** (multi-turn, tool calls, observations) rather than a response; the reward is **sparse and delayed** (did the task succeed, minutes and thousands of tokens later); and generation now depends on an **external, stateful, slow, failure-prone environment**. The first change is algorithmic bookkeeping, the second is a credit-assignment problem, and the third quietly dominates: agentic RL is 70% environment and infrastructure engineering, 30% learning algorithm. It is also where 2025–26's largest capability gains live — SWE agents, computer use, deep research — which is why every serious lab reorganized around it.

**Environment engineering is the new data engineering.** For single-turn RLVR you curated problems; here you build worlds: code repos with reproducible test harnesses (SWE-Gym; SWE-smith synthesizes them at scale), terminal/OS sandboxes, browsers, API/tool simulators (τ-bench-style customer-service worlds), search corpora (Search-R1), or your actual product. The environment defines the task distribution, the reward, and the exploit surface. Realism vs. hackability is the central tension: rich environments teach transferable behavior but hide reward bugs (an agent that learns to `rm` the failing test has solved *your environment*, not the task). NVIDIA's NeMo Gym-class suites and the community environment hubs (Prime Intellect's, `verifiers`-compatible envs) exist precisely because good environments are scarce, reusable assets.

## 9.2 The recipe

The proven path is **SFT on trajectories first, RL second** — RL from a model that can't reliably emit well-formed tool calls burns compute discovering syntax:

1. **Trajectory SFT / cold start.** Distill trajectories from a stronger agent (or your own model with heavy rejection sampling — keep only successful, verified trajectories), on your harness's exact tool/chat format. Kimi-Dev's lesson: even "agentless" structured pipelines make good skill priors for agent SFT.
2. **Agentic RL.** GRPO-family at the trajectory level: sample G full trajectories per task, reward = task success (+ small shaping: format validity, efficiency penalties), advantage per Chapter 7, with two agent-specific rules that are easy to get wrong:
   - **Mask environment/tool-output tokens from the loss** — the model must never receive gradient credit for text the environment produced (else it learns to hallucinate observations). The multi-turn analog of Chapter 4's masking rule, and the #1 agentic-framework bug historically.
   - **Credit assignment granularity:** trajectory-level advantage (every model token in the trajectory shares the outcome advantage) is the robust default; turn/step-level refinements (GiGPO-style grouped step advantages, per-turn value estimates, reasoning-reward models scoring intermediate steps) help on long horizons but add hackable machinery. Earn your way up.
3. **Iterate the environment as much as the policy** — inspect failed and *suspiciously successful* trajectories weekly; patch reward holes; grow task difficulty (curriculum by task success rate, exactly as in Chapter 8).

Reward design in practice: unit-test/goal-check outcome rewards wherever possible; rubric/judge rewards for fuzzy outcomes (research reports, conversations) with Chapter 6's audit discipline; resist dense hand-shaped step rewards — they are the most-hacked reward class in the RL literature and agents are superb at finding the degenerate optimum.

## 9.3 What it does to your infrastructure

Trajectory generation has *extreme* duration variance (one task finishes in 4 turns, another runs 200 tool calls and an hour of sandbox time), so the synchronous generate-batch-then-train loop of Chapter 8 collapses — stragglers idle the fleet. Consequences (mechanics in Chapter 12): **async/off-policy training becomes near-mandatory** (decoupled rollout and trainer pools, trajectories trained on a version or several behind, with importance-sampling/staleness corrections); **partial rollouts** (checkpoint a trajectory mid-flight, resume after a weight update); **environments as a service** (fleets of containerized sandboxes — Docker/Firecracker — with health checks, timeouts, seed/state reset, horizontal scaling; environment throughput, not GPU count, is the usual bottleneck); and observability per trajectory (full replayable traces), because your debugging unit is "watch what the agent did." Cost structure follows: inference + environment compute dominates; the gradient math is the cheap part.

## 9.4 Evaluation and expectations

Standard yardsticks: SWE-bench Verified (+ its live/rebench variants) for code agents, τ²-bench for tool-use/conversation, terminal-bench, OSWorld for computer use, plus deep-research benchmarks (BrowseComp-class) — all with Chapter 14's contamination and variance caveats, and all secondary to a held-out slice of *your own* task distribution. Expect: agentic SFT alone gets you a competent-but-brittle agent; RL reliably buys reliability and recovery behavior (retrying, re-planning after errors) on in-distribution tasks; cross-environment transfer is real but partial; and every point of reward improvement must be audited against the possibility that the agent found a hole in your world. The teams that win here are the ones that treat their environments as products.
