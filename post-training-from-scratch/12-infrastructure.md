# Chapter 12 — Infrastructure

## 12.1 Conceptual picture

SFT/DPO infrastructure is pretraining infrastructure in miniature — a distributed trainer, solved by Chapter 13's frameworks. RL is different in kind: it welds **two opposed systems into one loop** — a *trainer* (wants sharded weights, big synchronous batches, high-precision gradients) and an *inference engine* (wants replicated weights, continuous batching, low-precision speed) — plus a *reward layer* (verifiers, sandboxes, judges, environments) and an orchestrator moving data and weights between them. Generation dominates the budget: with long CoT, 60–90% of wall-clock is rollouts, so the design centers on keeping generation fed. Four decisions define any RL system: where the engines live (colocation), how weights move (sync), how off-policy you allow training to get (async), and how numerically consistent the two engines are (mismatch). Get these from a framework (Chapter 13) — but you cannot operate one without understanding them, because their failure modes surface as *learning* pathologies.

## 12.2 The four decisions

- **Rollout engine.** vLLM or SGLang, in-process with the trainer. Naive `model.generate()` is 10–100× too slow — paged KV cache and continuous batching are not optional. The engine also serves Best-of-N, judge/GenRM calls, and OPD teacher passes.
- **Colocated vs disaggregated.** *Colocated*: trainer and rollout engine time-share the same GPUs — weights resharded/offloaded between phases ("sleep/wake" memory choreography). Simple, no idle pools, the right default up to moderate scale; cost = phase-switch latency and peak-memory pressure. *Disaggregated*: separate GPU pools, streaming trajectories one way and weights the other. Buys concurrency (train batch N while generating N+k), independent scaling of the two fleets, heterogeneous hardware (rollouts on cheaper/preemptible GPUs) — and buys you async's staleness problem. 2026 large-scale practice is disaggregated or hybrid; verl exposes both.
- **Sync vs async.** Synchronous (fully on-policy: generate batch → update → repeat) wastes the fleet on the *longest* rollout in every batch — tolerable at 4k responses, crippling at 64k CoT, fatal for hour-long agent trajectories (Chapter 9). The async ladder: one-step-off pipelining → bounded-staleness pools (train on trajectories ≤s versions old) → fully async with **in-flight weight updates** (PipelineRL-style: update engine weights *mid-generation*, so one trajectory spans policies) and **partial rollouts** (pause/resume trajectories across updates; the Kimi K1.5-lineage trick). Every rung buys throughput and pays in off-policyness — corrected by Chapter 7's machinery (clipping, and truncated importance sampling per token, below). AReaL, slime, and the agentic frameworks live high on this ladder; start low.
- **Weight sync.** Every update must reach every rollout replica: NCCL broadcast / CUDA-IPC when colocated (sub-second to seconds at 8–70B), RDMA transfers cross-pool, bucketed per-tensor with resharding (trainer TP/FSDP layout → inference TP layout) handled by the framework. At MoE scale this is a serious engineering line item (and FP8-quantized sync is now common to halve it). Sync latency directly multiplies staleness.

## 12.3 Train–inference mismatch — the silent killer

Your rollout engine and trainer compute *different numbers* for the same tokens: different kernels, different reduction orders, batch-variant kernels, different precisions (BF16 everywhere still disagrees; FP8 rollouts more so). So the sampled tokens' logprobs under the trainer differ from the logprobs they were sampled with — you are **secretly off-policy even when "on-policy"**, and at long response lengths the per-token discrepancies compound into genuine divergence: reward curves that mysteriously flatline or collapse, especially on MoE (where routing amplifies tiny numeric drift; part of why GSPO's sequence-level treatment helps). Defenses, all standard by 2026: **monitor the logprob gap** (mean |Δlogprob| between engine and trainer on sampled tokens — the single most valuable RL-infra metric; alert on drift), apply **truncated importance sampling** (reweight each token by min(π_train/π_engine, C), C≈2 — cheap and dramatically stabilizing), pin engine/trainer versions, and validate any FP8-rollout config against a BF16 baseline before trusting it. If your framework doesn't expose the logprob gap, add it before your first real run.

## 12.4 The reward layer and the operational floor

Verifiers and environments are production services, not scripts: **sandboxed execution** for model-written code (containers/microVMs — Firecracker/gVisor at scale — no network, CPU/mem/time limits; the model *will* emit fork bombs and `rm -rf` by exploration), horizontally scaled (code-RL needs thousands of executions/minute; agentic RL needs thousands of *stateful* environments — Chapter 9), health-checked and timeout-wrapped (a hung verifier stalls the loop; a *crashed* verifier that returns 0.0 silently poisons it — distinguish "wrong" from "errored" in your reward schema and drop errored samples). Judge/GenRM rewards add an inference fleet of their own.

Operational floor for any serious run: checkpoint the *whole loop state* (policy, optimizer, data-position, RNG, in-flight rollout buffer — resume must not replay or skip prompts); log **full transcripts** of a rollout sample every step (you will read them — Chapters 6, 8, 16); dashboard the Chapter 7 signal set (reward, length, entropy, clip fraction, logprob gap, verifier error rate, rollout/train utilization split); expect end-to-end "MFU" of 10–25% and optimize the *rollout* side first (it's where the time is). Reproducibility is worse than pretraining — batch-variant kernels and async make exact reruns near-impossible — so rely on seeds + logged transcripts + tight metric tracking rather than bitwise determinism.

## 12.5 Decisions

≤8 GPUs, short responses: colocated, synchronous, TRL-class stack — spend your effort on data and evals. 16–128 GPUs, long-CoT RLVR: verl-class hybrid, colocated to start, TIS on from day one, logprob-gap dashboard before first run. Agentic or 64k-token regimes: disaggregated + bounded-staleness async + partial rollouts, environments as a managed fleet. Any scale: sandbox all execution, schema-separate verifier errors from zero rewards, and read transcripts daily.
