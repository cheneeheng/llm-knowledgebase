# Chapter 11 — Safety, Alignment, and Character

## 11.1 Conceptual picture

Safety post-training decides what the model will and won't do, and *who it is* while doing it. It is not a final filter bolted on before release; in every serious pipeline it is a workstream threaded through all the preceding chapters — refusal demonstrations in the SFT mix, safety preferences in DPO/RM data, safety rewards in RL, safety evals in every regression gate. Two truths hold it in tension: safety behaviors are among the *easiest* things to train (a few thousand examples move refusal behavior dramatically — and, symmetrically, a small hostile fine-tune can strip it; fine-tuning access is itself a safety surface), and they are among the easiest to get *wrong in both directions* — under-refusing harm and over-refusing legitimate requests are both product failures, and naive training reliably buys one by selling the other. Measure both axes, always (harm benches *and* over-refusal sets like XSTest/OR-Bench).

## 11.2 The policy layer: specs and constitutions

Before training anything, you need a written definition of intended behavior. The frontier labs converged on public **model specs**: OpenAI's Model Spec, Anthropic's constitution for Claude (substantially revised January 2026 toward *reason-based* rather than rule-based guidance — explaining the "why" behind each behavior, so models can generalize to unenumerated cases). Write your own, even if it's three pages: taxonomy of harms with in/out-of-scope examples, tone under refusal (refuse-and-redirect beats lecture), gray-zone policy (dual-use questions, roleplay, medical/legal). Every downstream data decision — what annotators label, what the judge rewards, what red-teamers probe — derives from this document. Without it, your safety behavior is whatever your cheapest data vendor thought it should be.

## 11.3 The training techniques

- **Safety SFT + preference data.** Curated refusals and safe-completions in the SFT mix (a few % suffices); harmlessness pairs in DPO/RM data (chosen = helpful-and-safe or well-toned refusal). Balance with adversarial *benign* prompts (scary-sounding-but-legitimate) or you train keyword-triggered over-refusal.
- **Constitutional AI / RLAIF.** Replace most human safety labels with AI feedback steered by explicit principles: model critiques and revises its own outputs against the constitution (revision pairs feed SFT), and an AI judge applying the constitution labels preference pairs (feeding the RM/DPO). This is how safety data scales — humans write *principles* and audit; models generate the volume. It is also the general template for value-shaping beyond safety.
- **Deliberative alignment** (the reasoning-era upgrade, OpenAI's o-series): teach the model the *text of the spec itself* and train it — via SFT on spec-citing CoT, then RL with a spec-grading judge — to reason over the policy at inference time when a request is borderline. Materially better than pattern-matched refusals on jailbreak robustness and over-refusal simultaneously; the same idea transfers to any thinking model (STAR-1 showed a 1k-example version helps open reasoning models).
- **Character training.** The model's persona — warmth, honesty, epistemic humility, how it disagrees — is trained the same way (constitution-style trait lists → self-generated preference data), not left to the system prompt. Anthropic pioneered treating character as a first-class alignment target; practically, decide your assistant's traits in the spec and generate trait-consistency preference data for them. Sycophancy is the anti-character to fight explicitly: human-preference data *rewards* agreement, so add counter-sycophancy pairs (chosen = polite disagreement when the user is wrong) or your RLHF stage will train a flatterer.
- **Safety for agents** is its own layer: refusal training on harmful *tasks* (AgentHarm-class), prompt-injection robustness (train on injected observations, reward ignoring them — tool outputs are attacker-controlled input), and hard scaffolding limits (sandboxing, allowlists, confirmation gates) that do not rely on the model at all. Defense in depth: weights are one layer, never the only one.

## 11.4 Red-teaming and the release gate

Red-teaming is the adversarial half of safety training: humans (expert + crowdsourced) and automated attackers (attacker LLMs, jailbreak-mutation suites, gradient-free search) hunting for policy violations; every found failure becomes training data for the next round (the failure-driven loop from Chapter 3's data machinery). Standard practice for a serious release: internal red-team pass on the frozen candidate, jailbreak benches (in-house + public), harm/over-refusal benches, sycophancy and bias evals, dual-use capability evals if you're anywhere near frontier scale (bio/cyber uplift — the labs' responsible-scaling frameworks govern these), and a written go/no-go with safety regressions treated as release blockers, not footnotes. Post-launch: monitor, collect novel attacks, fold back into data. Safety training is a loop with the adversary, and the adversary ships after you do.

## 11.5 The alignment tax, honestly

Safety training costs capability only when done crudely (blanket refusal data → over-refusal and dumber edge-case behavior). The 2026 toolkit — reason-based specs, deliberative alignment, counter-balanced data, OPD-based capability recovery after safety stages (Chapter 10) — keeps the measured tax near zero on standard capability evals. What it genuinely costs is *engineering attention*: the safety workstream is roughly a fifth of a competent post-training effort. Teams that skip it don't ship faster; they ship a liability and retrofit under fire.
