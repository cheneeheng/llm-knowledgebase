# Chapter 8 — RLVR and Thinking Models

## 8.1 Conceptual picture

RLVR — reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards — is Chapter 7's loop pointed at tasks where correctness is *checkable*: math with known answers, code with tests, constraints with validators. The reward is (nearly) unhackable relative to a reward model, so you can optimize hard, for a long time, without the RM's overoptimization ceiling. Do that on a strong base model and something qualitative happens: responses grow from hundreds to thousands of tokens as the model learns to *think* — decompose, try approaches, check itself, backtrack ("wait, let me reconsider") — because thinking longer is reinforced whenever it flips failures to successes. That is the entire thinking-model recipe: o1 demonstrated it, DeepSeek-R1 published it, and everything since is refinement.

Is RL creating capability or eliciting it? The honest 2026 answer: mostly elicitation, with real exceptions. The pass@k critique: RLVR-trained models beat their base at pass@1 but the *base* often matches or wins at pass@256 — RL concentrates probability mass on reasoning paths the base already could sample, trading diversity for reliability. Counter-evidence (ProRL-line work) shows prolonged, well-regularized RL on broad task mixes expanding the reachable set outright, especially where the base was weak. Practical planning rule stands: **your base model's latent ability caps your RL run** — pick the base for the target (Chapter 2), and treat "RL will teach it X from zero" as a research bet, not a plan.

## 8.2 The R1 recipe as the reference template

DeepSeek-R1's four stages remain the canonical full pipeline: (1) **cold-start SFT** — a few thousand curated long-CoT traces, so RL amplifies a coherent behavior instead of discovering it from raw scratch (R1-Zero proved raw-from-base *works* — emergent reflection, the "aha moment" — but yields unreadable, language-mixed traces); (2) **reasoning RL** — large-scale GRPO on math/code/logic with rule-based rewards plus format and language-consistency rewards; (3) **rejection-sampling SFT** — harvest ~600k verified reasoning traces from the RL'd model, blend ~200k general data, re-SFT for breadth; (4) **general RL** — combined verifiable + preference rewards to restore all-around behavior. Qwen 3's variant adds a **thinking-mode fusion** stage (one model, `/think` and `/no_think` behaviors) and closes with general RLHF. Two failure-shaped lessons embedded there: pure reasoning RL *narrows* a model (stage 3–4 exist to buy back generality), and format/language rewards exist because RL will otherwise drift into private notation.

## 8.3 Verifiers, data, curriculum

- **Verifiers are load-bearing.** False positives are worse than false negatives: the policy *finds* every grader bug and amplifies it into a strategy (printing expected outputs, exploiting weak test suites, answer-format tricks). Use hardened equivalence checkers for math (math-verify-class, not exact string match), mutation-tested test suites for code, sandboxed execution with time/memory limits (Chapter 12). Audit by reading high-reward transcripts and by holding out a stricter secondary verifier; a growing gap between primary and holdout reward is hacking, caught early.
- **Reward shape:** binary correctness dominates; add small format rewards early (parseable answer, `<think>` structure), a language-consistency reward if traces drift multilingual, and an overlong penalty (DAPO's soft length cap) rather than a hard truncation cliff.
- **Difficulty curriculum = pass-rate filtering.** Estimate the current policy's pass rate per prompt; train on prompts with 0 < pass < 1 (DAPO's dynamic sampling automates the batch-level version); re-estimate and refresh as the model improves — a static dataset silently degenerates into all-solved + all-impossible, and reward "plateaus" that are really data exhaustion. Dataset scale: serious open runs use 10⁴–10⁵ *good* prompts (quality/difficulty spread matters far more than count); DeepScaleR-class results at 1.5B came from ~40k problems and careful staging.
- **Staged context growth** is standard: e.g., 8k → 16k → 24k max response length as the model earns it (compute follows length; don't pay 32k prices for 4k thoughts).

## 8.4 Length, entropy, and the two collapses

Response length is your most information-dense training curve. Healthy runs: length grows in punctuated ramps as new behaviors (verification, backtracking) get reinforced. Pathologies: **length explosion without reward gain** (length-biased advantage — use Dr. GRPO/DAPO corrections; or the model padding to avoid a wrong answer under a truncation penalty) and **length collapse** (overlong penalty too sharp, or entropy collapse below). Post-training length control, if you need it: budget forcing (s1's "wait"-appending trick at inference), length-penalized RL fine-tuning, or dual-mode training à la Qwen 3.

**Entropy collapse** is the characteristic RLVR failure: token entropy crashes, all G samples per prompt become near-identical, exploration dies, learning stops — often *while pass@1 still looks fine*. Levers, in order: clip-higher (DAPO's asymmetric clipping preserves probability-raising updates on rare tokens), dynamic sampling, temperature ~1.0, no/lower KL-to-reference, and data refresh (exhausted data accelerates collapse). Watch pass@k: it falls before anything else tells you. The opposite failure — entropy stuck high, no learning — usually means your reward is all-zero/all-one (difficulty filter broken) or your verifier is returning noise.

## 8.5 Distillation interacts (and often wins)

R1's most practically important table: its RL recipe applied directly to small models *lost* to simply SFT-distilling R1's traces into them — small models lack the substrate for RL discovery but imitate discovered reasoning beautifully. Corollaries: below ~7B, distill from a thinking teacher (yours or open) rather than running RLVR; at any size, a distilled cold start from a stronger reasoner beats a from-scratch one; and a modest RL polish *on top of* distillation reliably adds a few points (the standard 2026 small-model recipe, increasingly via on-policy distillation — Chapter 10). Run RLVR from scratch only on models big and well-pretrained enough to explore.

## 8.6 Compute and expectations

RLVR compute is modest next to pretraining but no longer trivial: open 7–32B reasoning runs land in the 10³–10⁵ H100-hour range depending on response length and iteration count (generation dominates — Chapter 12); frontier labs have scaled RL by orders of magnitude since o1 and now treat RL compute as a first-class budget line rivaling pretraining. Expect: large gains on in-domain verifiable tasks (math competition scores moving 20–40 points is real), moderate transfer to adjacent reasoning, near-zero transfer to unrelated chat quality (that's Chapters 5–6's job), and a general-capability *regression* if you skip the generality-restoring stages. Measure all four.
