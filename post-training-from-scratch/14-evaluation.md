# Chapter 14 — Evaluation

## 14.1 Conceptual picture

Evaluation is not the report card at the end; it is the steering wheel. Post-training's tight loops mean you evaluate dozens of checkpoints per week, and whatever your evals measure is what your pipeline optimizes — including their bugs (a length-biased judge trains verbose models; a contaminated benchmark trains false confidence). Build the eval stack *before* the first training run (Chapter 2.4), hold parts of it out from every optimization loop, and treat eval engineering with the same rigor as training. Four layers, cheapest to most expensive: automated benchmarks, LLM-as-judge, humans, and production telemetry.

## 14.2 The benchmark layer

Assemble a fixed **capability panel** run on every candidate: knowledge/reasoning (MMLU-Pro, GPQA-Diamond), math (MATH-500, AIME by-year), code (LiveCodeBench, HumanEval+ as smoke test), instruction following (IFEval, Multi-IF), long context (RULER-class), chat quality (Arena-Hard, AlpacaEval-2-LC), agentic if relevant (SWE-bench Verified, τ²-bench, terminal-bench, OSWorld), safety (harm benches + XSTest/OR-Bench over-refusal + sycophancy evals), plus — most importantly — **your own held-out product distribution**, which is the only benchmark nobody else has gamed. Mechanics matter as much as selection: fix the harness version, chat template, sampling params, and n; report `pass@1 (n=k)` averaged over k samples for small/noisy sets (AIME is 30 questions — single-sample scores are coin flips; n=32 is standard); pin judge model versions; and never compare numbers across different harnesses (the same model scores points apart between harnesses for boring formatting reasons).

Contamination discipline: decontaminate training data against the panel (Chapter 3.5) — and remember **RL contaminates via prompts alone** (a model RL-trained on benchmark-adjacent prompts inflates even with no leaked answers); prefer *live/rolling* benchmarks (LiveCodeBench windows, AIME by year, fresh HLE-class sets) for headline claims; treat any suspicious jump on a static benchmark as contamination until proven otherwise.

## 14.3 LLM-as-judge, and its biases

Judges (pairwise preference or rubric scoring by a strong model) are the workhorse for open-ended quality — cheap enough to run per-checkpoint, correlated enough with humans to steer by. But every judge bias becomes a training-side incentive, so control them mechanically, not hopefully: **position bias** (swap orders, average), **verbosity bias** (length-controlled metrics — AlpacaEval-2-LC's correction is the model; report length deltas alongside win rates), **self-preference** (never judge with the model family being trained; ensemble judges for anything load-bearing), **style-over-substance** (rubric- or reference-guided grading for factual tasks; style-controlled comparisons à la LMArena). Calibrate the judge itself once against a few hundred human labels from your distribution; re-calibrate when you change judge model or prompt. Human evaluation then gets reserved for what judges do worst — taste, trust, domain expertise — and for periodically auditing the judges. Public arenas (LMArena) are useful weak signal for flagships; they are unreproducible, style-sensitive, and gameable, so never a training target.

## 14.4 Training-time evaluation: the RL dashboard

During RL, evals are a *live instrument*, not a postmortem (the signal set from Chapters 7–8, consolidated): reward on train and on a **held-out prompt set** (gap = overfitting/hacking); response length (explained moves only); token entropy and **pass@k** (k=8–64) on a fixed probe set — pass@1 rising while pass@k falls is diversity collapse, invisible to every leaderboard; clip fraction and logprob gap (Chapter 12); verifier error rates; and a **holdout grader** the loop never optimizes (stricter verifier, different judge) whose divergence from the training reward is your earliest reward-hacking alarm. Plus the non-negotiable qualitative loop: read a fixed sample of transcripts at every eval point — top-reward, bottom-reward, and random. Every serious failure mode in Chapter 16 was caught by someone reading transcripts, not by a metric.

## 14.5 Statistical hygiene and the regression gate

Small evals are noisy, post-training deltas are often 1–3 points, and RL runs vary by seed — so: multiple seeds for load-bearing comparisons (or at minimum multiple eval samples), confidence intervals on everything (bootstrap over questions; a ±2.5pt CI on a 200-question set dwarfs most "improvements"), paired comparisons on identical questions rather than aggregate-vs-aggregate, and pre-registered decision criteria before launching an experiment (which metric must move, by how much, what breaks the tie). Finally, formalize the **release gate**: the fixed panel + safety suite + regression set of curated "must not break" behaviors (formatting, tool syntax, refusal tone, known past bugs) that every shipping candidate passes; anything less and your pipeline's dozens of weekly checkpoints will eventually ship you a regression you already fixed once.
